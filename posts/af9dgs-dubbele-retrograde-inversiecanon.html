Double retrograde inversion canon, a 'square' of music that can be played from all 4 sides in canon

https://musescore.com/user/6168221/scores/4812072

A table canon (crab canon/retrograde inversion canon) is meant to be placed on a table in between two musicians, who both read the same music in opposing directions. This notion can be extended to 2 dimensions where you have a square of (in the example) 4x4 bars and 2 additional players also play the columns as a retrograde inversion canon.

What do you guys think? I've found this very difficult to write, I'd love to have some open discussion on how to construct and work on these canons to see if a truly musical variant can be constructed. Anybody have experience with retrograde canons?

See the example, completely tonal and following the rules of counterpoint.

You read per bar, imagine a square of 3x3 bars, 1 trough 9, you would simply read the bars in this order: 1 4 7 2 5 8 3 6 9.

I'll refrain from writing a whole essay on it, but one interesting thing when writing retrograde inversion canons in half notes is that your weak and strong beats get reversed. This is awkward at first, but it's actually a blessing in disguise as the "unimportant" notes of one voice are the important ones of the other and vice-versa. This generalizes to runs of quarters and eights, in fact writing just in eights is a very nice way to write retrograde inversion canons.

The table in the post is hard :P, but a table generally is more about reading in columns and simple 3x3 2 voice (one normal, one in columns) are quite alright and you get interesting material chopping up your music like that.

There are some problems with having exactly those kinds of rules as the interaction depends not only on the time and pitch offset but also on the duration.

For first species at an agreed upon count of bars with no time or pitch transformation you can make these rules (but not improvise with them, because the other singer needs to know the material before hand in order to start at the end), but they would be more complicated than regular canons. The biggest "problem" is that some elegance is lost because effectively the whole first half can be freely written and subsequently accompanied. Said differently, any two part first species counterpoint can be converted to a retrograde inversion canon by taking the bottom voice and appending it in retrograde inversion to the top voice (as first species is (mostly...) symmetrical over time direction).

Yea definitely very constrained, so I don't write in linearly. I start with some important points a cadence here, some motive there and fill in the other voices. Then I build up with much backtracking at each point. I obviously start with a first species like solution before adding subdivisions (often to solve problems) , it seems too intimidating otherwise.

There were fundamentally 2 problems that I could practice separately, writing retrograde inversions canons, and writing material that is readable in columns. Of retrograde inversion canons I just wrote enough to feel somewhat comfortable with the transformation. For the columns I started with 3x3 2 voice to practice column reading + straight reading (they are quite easy and quick to write). And later 4x4 in more voices (like this one: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QRWnSrANjK8)

Right now the solution is essentially second species-ish, flourid counterpoint will sound better and offer far more solutions to the constraints, but is much harder to keep straight. So yea there are many solutions.
