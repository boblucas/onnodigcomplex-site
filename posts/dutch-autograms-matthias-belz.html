The reason for the plurals is that (in principle) words ending on vowel sounds get -s, otherwise -en. But then also normally abbrv. always get -s and so you get this complicated mix. Also words ending is -ts are so common that "z's" sounds quite natural.

But some things are still clearly wrong, s's is just not Dutch (https://woordenlijst.org/zoeken/?q=s%27en) and neither is say a'en https://woordenlijst.org/zoeken/?q=a%27s.

I looked at some frequencies in 3 sources of Dutch text that show how dynamic the situation is, please don't feel the need to look at it in detail, but it's interesting to see how things change over time! We can explain pretty clearly which words in the woordenlijst.org allow both options and which don't, using the frequencies. And mixing and matching is common (I can list examples if you want, but you know...)

Finally with regards to mixing and matching I would not mix and match within l and m, it's either l's, m's or l'en en m'en. Because those letters have the same exact form. Hi!

Thanks! And your introductory paragraph is completely correct!

I personally consider all letters that allow both suffixes to be of the same group z excluded. But that's pretty personal.

To try and justify it: the vowels are just pronounced as themselves, and must be followed by an -s (also per woordenlijst.org) (do note that y is a vowel, /ɛɪ/). Next there are consonants succeeded by a vowel, mostly 'e', and they must also use -s (also per woordenlijst.org) there is [bcdgjptvw]e (like /beː/), [hk]a (/kaː/) and qu (/kyː/). Finally also fixed are s (ɛs) and x (ɪks), they must use -en, as they end on an s sound, again see woordenlijst.org

For the ambigous cases there are consonants precended by a vowel and they all have the same form: /ɛ[flmnr]/. And so it seems to me z as /zɛt/ is the odd one out, hence I can choose it independently and it still 'feels' right.

Now if somebody does mix and match and just says "yea ɛfs feels better, but so does ɛnən" I would find the solution unsatisfying, but I mean, is the logic above really strong enough to forbid it? You tell me.
